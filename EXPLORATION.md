# Mathematics Münster — Research Objectives & Topic Groups

Source: <https://www.uni-muenster.de/MathematicsMuenster/aboutus/objectives.shtml>

Mathematics Münster (MM) organises its research around the idea that
mathematics is **one connected organism**. Rather than working in isolated
specialities, they deliberately transfer methods, ideas, and knowledge between
fields. The research is grouped into **8 research objectives (O1–O8)** that
contain **10 topic groups**, plus **2 structural objectives (O9–O10)** about how
the centre operates.

Below: each topic group, its key concepts, and a plain explanation aimed at an
undergraduate who has seen basic algebra, analysis, topology, and probability.

---

## O1 — Push frontiers in K-theory and attack open questions in topology

### Topic 1: K-groups and cohomology

**Key concepts**

- **Cohomology theory** — A machine that assigns algebraic objects (groups) to a
  space, measuring "holes" of different dimensions. If two spaces have different
  cohomology, they cannot be the same shape. Think of it as a refined version of
  counting connected components, loops, voids, etc.
- **K-theory** — A special, powerful cohomology theory. Instead of counting
  holes, it organises information about **vector bundles** (families of vector
  spaces attached continuously to each point of a space) or about projective
  modules over a ring. It packages this into groups called **K-groups**.
- **Farrell–Jones conjecture** — A deep prediction that the K-theory of a group
  can be computed from simpler pieces (its finite and virtually-cyclic
  subgroups). Proving new cases gives leverage on many other problems.
- **Borel conjecture (topological rigidity)** — Claims that certain "nice"
  manifolds (aspherical ones) are completely determined by their fundamental
  group: if two such manifolds have the same fundamental group, they are
  actually the same up to continuous deformation (homeomorphic).
- **C\*-algebra classification** — Using K-theory as a fingerprint to tell
  infinite-dimensional algebras of operators apart.

**Plain explanation.** This group builds and computes algebraic "fingerprints"
of spaces and algebras. K-theory is the central fingerprint. They use it to
decide when two shapes are really the same, to bound the curvature of manifolds,
and to classify operator algebras.

## O2 — Study moduli spaces to advance the Langlands programme and other fields

### Topic 2: Moduli spaces in arithmetic and geometry

**Key concepts**

- **Moduli space** — A space whose *points are themselves mathematical objects*.
  For example, one point might be "an elliptic curve" or "a Riemann surface";
  moving around the moduli space deforms the object. It lets you study a whole
  family at once and ask geometric questions about the family.
- **Langlands programme** — A vast web of conjectures linking number theory
  (solutions of equations, symmetries of numbers / Galois groups) with analysis
  and representation theory (automorphic forms). Often called a "grand unified
  theory" of mathematics.
- **Representation (of a group)** — A way of writing abstract group elements as
  matrices, turning symmetry into linear algebra you can compute with.
- **Diffeomorphism groups** — The group of all smooth, smoothly-invertible
  deformations of a manifold; understanding it reveals how flexible a high-
  dimensional shape is.
- **Stable curves / Strebel differentials** — Specific well-behaved objects
  whose moduli spaces appear in geometry and mathematical physics.

**Plain explanation.** Instead of studying one curve or one geometric object,
they study the *space of all of them*. The arithmetic of these spaces feeds the
Langlands programme; their geometry feeds topology, differential geometry, and
physics.

## O3 — Open questions in C\*-algebras, model building, and set theory

### Topic 3: Models and universes

**Key concepts**

- **Model (in logic) / universe of sets** — A mathematical world that satisfies
  the axioms of set theory. There can be many such worlds, and statements true
  in one may fail in another.
- **Large cardinal hypotheses** — Assumptions that extremely huge infinite
  numbers exist. They sit at the top of the strength hierarchy of mathematics
  and have surprising consequences far below.
- **Determinacy** — A statement that certain infinite two-player games always
  have a winning strategy for one side. It interacts deeply with large cardinals
  and with how "nice" sets of real numbers behave.

**Plain explanation.** This group builds different logical universes to test
which mathematical statements are provable, independent, or require strong
infinity assumptions — probing the boundaries of what mathematics can decide.

### Topic 4: Groups and actions

**Key concepts**

- **Group action** — A group acting as symmetries of a space (each group element
  moves the space around in a structure-preserving way). The way a group acts
  reveals properties of both the group and the space.
- **C\*-algebra** — An algebra of operators on a Hilbert space, closed under a
  conjugation/adjoint operation and complete in a norm. These are the natural
  language of quantum mechanics and a meeting point of algebra and analysis.
- **Cartan subalgebra** — A well-behaved commutative "coordinate system" sitting
  inside a larger non-commutative algebra; finding one makes the big algebra far
  easier to understand and classify.
- **Nuclear / simple C\*-algebras** — Technical "good behaviour" conditions
  ("nuclear" = approximable by finite-dimensional pieces; "simple" = no nontrivial
  closed ideals).
- **Tools: functional analysis, probability, combinatorics** — Different toolkits
  combined to study groups and their actions.

**Plain explanation.** They study symmetry (groups) acting on operator algebras,
trying to prove these algebras contain hidden commutative skeletons (Cartan
subalgebras) that allow a full classification.

## O4 — PDEs and differential geometry via method transfer

### Topic 5: Curvature, shape and global analysis

**Key concepts**

- **Riemannian manifold** — A smooth curved space equipped with a notion of
  distance and angle at every point (a generalised surface in any dimension).
- **Curvature** — A local measurement of how a space bends (positive like a
  sphere, zero like a flat plane, negative like a saddle).
- **Local vs. global** — How local curvature forces global shape: the central
  theme of "global analysis". E.g. constant positive curvature forces the space
  to look like a sphere.
- **Einstein manifolds** — Manifolds whose curvature satisfies the same equation
  that governs gravity in general relativity.
- **Isometry group / symmetry** — Transformations preserving distances; assuming
  symmetry simplifies classification, and the goal is to remove those
  assumptions step by step (the **Grove programme**).

**Plain explanation.** They ask: how much does *local* bending control the
*overall* shape of a space? They aim to classify highly curved or Einstein
spaces, gradually dropping symmetry assumptions until none remain.

### Topic 6: Singularities and partial differential equations

**Key concepts**

- **Partial differential equation (PDE)** — An equation relating a function to
  its rates of change in several variables; PDEs model heat, waves, fluids,
  gravity, and more.
- **Singularity** — A point where a solution blows up or loses smoothness (e.g.
  a shock wave or a black hole). Understanding singularities is often the hardest
  part of a PDE.
- **Rigidity phenomena** — Situations where a small set of constraints forces a
  unique or very restricted solution.
- **Lorentzian / Einstein manifolds & general relativity** — The geometric
  setting of spacetime; Einstein's equations are the governing PDEs.
- **Stability of the Kerr black hole** — Whether a rotating black hole, when
  slightly perturbed, settles back down rather than falling apart — a major open
  problem in mathematical relativity.

**Plain explanation.** They study equations describing change and the points
where solutions break down, including the geometry of spacetime and whether
black holes are stable under disturbance.

## O5 — Stochastic analysis for non-commutative field theories

### Topic 7: Field theory and randomness

**Key concepts**

- **Quantum field theory (QFT)** — The framework describing particles and forces
  as excitations of fields; making it mathematically rigorous is a famous
  challenge.
- **Stochastic analysis** — Calculus for random functions, e.g. the random,
  jagged paths of Brownian motion.
- **Stochastic quantisation** — A technique that builds a quantum field theory as
  the long-time equilibrium of a random (noise-driven) evolution.
- **Free probability** — A "non-commutative" probability theory where random
  variables are operators that don't commute (order of multiplication matters),
  ideal for large random matrices.
- **Non-commutative space** — A space described by an algebra where `ab ≠ ba`,
  generalising ordinary geometry the way quantum mechanics generalises classical
  mechanics.
- **Critical vs. sub-critical dimension** — The dimension where a theory is
  hardest to control (critical) versus easier (sub-critical).

**Plain explanation.** They use randomness and non-commutative probability to
rigorously *construct* quantum field theories on exotic "non-commutative"
spaces — a goal physicists assume but mathematicians have not fully proven.

## O6 — Structures and their limits in stochastic models

### Topic 8: Random discrete structures and their limits

**Key concepts**

- **Random graph / network** — A graph whose edges are placed by chance; models
  social networks, the internet, etc. One studies typical, not worst-case,
  behaviour.
- **Random tessellation** — A random tiling of space into cells (e.g. cracks in
  dried mud), used in stochastic geometry.
- **Percolation** — A model of random connectivity (open/closed links); famous
  for a sharp **phase transition** where a giant connected cluster suddenly
  appears as the link probability crosses a threshold.
- **Interacting particle systems** — Many simple random agents whose local rules
  produce complex collective behaviour.
- **Scaling limit** — The clean, often universal object you get when you zoom out
  from a huge random discrete structure (analogous to how many random sums
  approach the bell curve).

**Plain explanation.** They study large random networks, tilings, and particle
systems, and the smooth limiting shapes that emerge when these structures grow
very large.

## O7 — Structures in multiscale processes; effective models

### Topic 9: Multiscale processes and effective behaviour

**Key concepts**

- **Multiscale phenomena** — Systems where tiny-scale details (molecules, fine
  material structure) affect large-scale behaviour (bulk properties).
- **Effective / homogenised model** — A simplified equation that captures the
  large-scale behaviour without tracking every small detail; "averaging out" the
  fine scale.
- **Scaling limit** — The mathematically rigorous passage from the detailed model
  to the effective one.
- **Optimal control & PDE-constrained optimisation** — Finding the best way to
  steer a system governed by differential equations (e.g. minimal-energy
  control).
- **Convergence rates** — Quantifying *how fast* an approximation approaches the
  true answer — essential for trustworthy numerics.

**Plain explanation.** Many systems mix small and large scales. They derive
simpler "effective" equations valid at the large scale, prove how accurate these
approximations are, and solve optimisation/control problems on top of them.

## O8 — Innovative simulation via model- and data-based approaches

### Topic 10: Deep learning and surrogate methods

**Key concepts**

- **Artificial neural network** — A flexible function built from layered
  weighted sums and simple non-linearities, tuned to fit data; the basis of
  modern machine learning.
- **Stochastic gradient descent (SGD)** — The randomised optimisation algorithm
  that "trains" a network by repeatedly nudging its parameters downhill on a
  loss function using random data samples.
- **Model order reduction** — Replacing a huge, expensive simulation with a
  small, fast one that keeps the essential behaviour.
- **Surrogate model** — A cheap approximation that stands in for a costly
  computation; "multi-fidelity" means combining cheap-but-rough and
  expensive-but-accurate models.
- **Parametric (S)PDE** — A PDE (possibly with randomness, "S" = stochastic)
  depending on parameters, which must be solved repeatedly for many parameter
  values.
- **Inverse problem** — Inferring hidden causes/parameters from observed data
  (the reverse of simulating forward).
- **Certified method** — One that comes with rigorous, provable error bounds, not
  just empirical accuracy.

**Plain explanation.** They put machine learning on a rigorous mathematical
footing — analysing why training works and combining data-driven networks with
classical models to build fast, *provably accurate* simulators for physics and
engineering.

## Structural objectives (how the centre operates)

### O9 — Integrated approach and international visibility

Treat mathematics as one connected whole; deliberately build bridges between the
topics above; grow into an internationally recognised research centre via
cooperations, visitor programmes, and the Centre for Mathematics Münster.

### O10 — Early-career researchers, equity and diversity

Attract and support top young mathematicians through the Graduate School and
career programmes, foster early scientific independence, and build an inclusive,
diverse research environment.

## Big picture for undergraduates

The recurring threads across these ten topic groups are:

- **Invariants/fingerprints** (K-theory, cohomology) that tell objects apart.
- **Moduli spaces** that study *all* objects of a type at once.
- **Local-to-global** principles (curvature controlling shape; PDE singularities).
- **Randomness** as a tool and an object (random graphs, stochastic
  quantisation, SGD).
- **Scaling limits / effective models** that extract simple behaviour from
  complex systems.
- **Method transfer** — the deliberate reuse of a technique from one field to
  crack a problem in another, which is the core philosophy of Mathematics
  Münster.
